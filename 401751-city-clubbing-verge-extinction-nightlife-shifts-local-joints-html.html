City clubbing on verge of extinction as nightlife shifts to local joints

April 2024 · 4 minute read

- Like ill-prepared soldiers being felled by enemy bullets, one-time giant clubs in Nairobi’s CBD keep taking the bow one after the other

- This has created an avenue for new establishments to come up in the estates, shifting the once vibrant nightlife away from town

- The shift has been attributed to insecurity, lower spending power and the night curfew imposed due to COVID-19

- Somehow, Sabina Joy remains the last man (woman?) standing

Florida 1 (Mad House), Simmers and recently, Mojos. Like ill-prepared soldiers in a war being felled by enemy bullets, the one-time giant clubs in Nairobi’s central business district keep taking the bow one after the other.

In its wake have mushroomed establishments that have taken the city's estates by storm, shifting the one-time bustling nightlife to the estates.

The big shift

TUKO.co.ke set out to investigate what could be driving the night club businesses out of the CBD, and whether the future of clubbing now sits neatly nestled in people’s neighborhoods.

According to Winnie Nduku, a long-standing reveler, estate clubs have this personal touch those in the CBD cannot match.

“Most times you will find that revelers in the estate clubs know each other, and it goes even for the waiters who serve food and drinks,” she said.

Winnie added that the availability of more space in estate clubs also gives a homely feeling, making partying more enjoyable than it was before.

“Even parking is easier to get. Sometimes we would park miles away from the club and walk all the way, then come back to retrieve the car while staggering and disoriented,” she added.

Winnie said cases of insecurity had also risen tremendously, so much that one would not enjoy, worried that their car was being vandalised or even stolen.

'Locals' are biggest beneficiaries

The shift was fueled further by the night curfew as most social drinkers sought joints near their houses where they would catch their drinks and still get home without getting into trouble with the law.

Edith Kagema, who runs a wines and spirits joint in Kilimani is thankful that crowds have been sent their direction as her joint was even forced to create a sitting area for patrons to sit and drink.

"Initially, people would just grab a bottle and disappear. These days they sit at the backyard and drink, we even had to start bringing live bands," she said.

According to Edith, the best thing about live bands coming to the people is that this is breaking tribal barriers that existed for a while.

Her explanation is that since an estate is a boiling pot for people from diverse ethnic backgrounds, an evening with Samidoh will still have local patrons who hardly understand the Gikuyu language he sings in enjoy his music.

She also attributed the shift to a low spending power as more people struggle to remain afloat financially.

"You see, the price of one beer in the CBD is not same as what is sold at the local. So if you have a pay cut but still want to drink you'll look for where the same can be found at a cheaper cost, and that's the local," she continued.

Forced to moonlight

James Avedi, a manager at a club in the CBD decried the loss of revenue saying they have been forced to reduce working hours and cash projections.

"Alcohol used to pay well in this CBD years ago. These days it's a struggle. We have even had to let go of some employees, others come in on a need-basis," he lamented.

The 35-year-old has been supplementing his drop in income by moonlighting as a middleman between sellers and would-be car buyers.

As the nightlife moves closer to the people, there have been complaints by some residents that the establishments play loud music, attract rowdy revelers and have brought call girls near residential places.

One such battle was between Kilimani's Space Lounge and the residents' association, with the club being forced to close its doors.

Despite the changing tides in the business environment, one joint has remained standing strong in the CBD; Sabina Joy. That's a story for another day.
